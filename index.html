Brynn Sherman

I am a cognitive neuroscientist broadly interested in learning and memory. The primary aim of my work is to understand how we use memories to form general knowledge, and how that knowledge in turn influences how new memories are formed. I am particularly interested in understanding the role of sleep in transforming our memories and facilitating new learning. In related lines of work, I also explore how acute stress influences different aspects of learning and memory. I use a range of methods to approach these questions, including behavior, fMRI, and intracranial EEG.

I am currently a post doc in Anna Schapiro's lab at the University of Pennsylvania. I completed my graduate work with Nick Turk-Browne at Yale, where I also collaborated closely with Elizabeth Goldfarb. Before graduate school, I was an undergraduate and lab manager at New York University, where I worked with Lila Davachi and Sarah DuBrow on how we perceive and remember time.

Outside of research, I like to spend my time cooking, baking, or exploring Philadelphia. I also enjoy playing (and modifying) board games.
